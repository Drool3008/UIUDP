# Literature foundations for MaayHealth: AI-first maternal nutrition for Indian mothers

This consolidated research deliverable for the MaayHealth UIUDP project at IIIT Hyderabad contains (1) a curated list of 20 verified academic papers with relevance notes, (2) a competitive app analysis table, and (3) a thematically organised literature review of ~1,150 words.

---

## 1. Curated academic papers (with relevance to MaayHealth)

The 20 papers below were selected from a larger verified set for direct relevance to MaayHealth's design pillars — voice-first, multilingual, AI-personalised maternal nutrition for Indian mothers.

### Theme 1 — mHealth for pregnancy and first-time mothers

**1. Murthy, N., Chandrasekharan, S., Prakash, M. P., Ganju, A., Peter, J., Kaonga, N. N., & Mechael, P. (2020).** *Effects of an mHealth voice message service (mMitra) on maternal health knowledge and practices of low-income women in India.* **BMC Public Health, 20, 820.** DOI: 10.1186/s12889-020-08965-2.
*Key finding:* In a Mumbai pseudo-RCT (n≈2,016), **91% of women rated mMitra voice messages "satisfactory/very satisfactory" and 92% useful**; care-seeking knowledge improved significantly, though some practice indicators did not.
*Relevance:* The canonical Indian validation of vernacular voice messaging — directly underwrites MaayHealth's voice-first, regional-language strategy, while its mixed practice outcomes motivate moving beyond passive content to interactive AI feedback.

**2. Lund, S., Nielsen, B. B., Hemed, M., et al. (2014).** *Mobile phones improve antenatal care attendance in Zanzibar: a cluster randomized controlled trial.* **BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 14, 29.** DOI: 10.1186/1471-2393-14-29.
*Key finding:* 44% of intervention vs. 31% of control women attended ≥4 ANC visits (OR 2.39) — the canonical citation for the often-quoted ~30%+ improvement in ANC adherence.
*Relevance:* Empirical foundation for MaayHealth's appointment-reminder and gestational-tracking modules.

**3. Bangal, V. B., Borawake, S. K., Gavhane, S. P., & Aher, K. H. (2017).** *Use of mobile phone for improvement in maternal health: a randomized control trial.* **Int. J. Reprod. Contracept. Obstet. Gynecol., 6(12).**
*Key finding:* Indian RCT (n=400) showed significantly higher ANC visits, IFA tablet consumption, tetanus immunisation, and institutional deliveries in the mobile-intervention arm.
*Relevance:* India-specific RCT proof that mobile interventions improve IFA compliance — a core MaayHealth nutrition outcome.

**4. Daly, L. M., Horey, D., Middleton, P. F., Boyle, F. M., & Flenady, V. (2018).** *The effect of mobile app interventions on influencing healthy maternal behavior and improving perinatal health outcomes: systematic review.* **JMIR mHealth and uHealth, 6(8), e10012.** DOI: 10.2196/10012.
*Key finding:* Across 4 RCTs, evidence is heterogeneous and underpowered; few outcomes reach statistical significance.
*Relevance:* Establishes the evaluation gap MaayHealth can address with rigorous outcome design.

**5. Hughson, J.-A. P., Daly, J. O., Woodward-Kron, R., Hajek, J., & Story, D. (2018).** *The rise of pregnancy apps and the implications for culturally and linguistically diverse women: narrative review.* **JMIR mHealth and uHealth, 6(11), e189.** DOI: 10.2196/mhealth.9119.
*Key finding:* Pregnancy apps overwhelmingly serve English-speaking, high-income, literate users; CALD women are systematically excluded.
*Relevance:* The single strongest critique motivating MaayHealth's vernacular and culturally-grounded design.

**6. Tucker, L., Villagomez, A. C., & Krishnamurti, T. (2021).** *Comprehensively addressing postpartum maternal health: a content and image review of commercially available mobile health apps.* **BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 21, 311.**
*Key finding:* Among 25 peripartum apps, the median covered only 19.5/30 postpartum maternal health topics; mothers of colour are poorly represented.
*Relevance:* The empirical basis for the "postpartum cliff" critique central to MaayHealth's positioning.

**7. Deave, T., Ginja, S., Goodenough, T., et al. (2019).** *The Bumps and BaBies Longitudinal Study (BaBBLeS): a multi-site cohort study of first-time mothers to evaluate the effectiveness of the Baby Buddy app.* **mHealth, 5, 42.**
*Key finding:* Baby Buddy did not significantly improve self-efficacy or wellbeing, but raised exclusive breastfeeding at 3 months (aOR 1.79); only 38.5% of users still engaged at 3 months.
*Relevance:* Cautionary engagement evidence — even NHS-backed video content struggles to retain first-time mothers without active personalisation.

**8. Mackintosh, N., Agarwal, S., Adcock, K., et al. (2022).** *First-time mothers' understanding and use of a pregnancy and parenting mobile app (Baby Buddy): qualitative study using appreciative inquiry.* **JMIR mHealth and uHealth, 10(11), e32757.**
*Key finding:* First-time mothers valued Baby Buddy as "foundational knowledge" because of NHS endorsement and professional video content.
*Relevance:* Validates trust-through-credentialed-content as a design lever MaayHealth must replicate (e.g., FOGSI/ICMR-vetted modules).

### Theme 2 — Cultural adaptation & language exclusion

**9. Resnicow, K., Baranowski, T., Ahluwalia, J. S., & Braithwaite, R. L. (1999).** *Cultural sensitivity in public health: defined and demystified.* **Ethnicity and Disease, 9(1), 10–21.**
*Key finding:* Distinguishes "surface" (language, food, imagery) from "deep" (values, beliefs, history) cultural adaptation; surface drives acceptance, deep drives behaviour change.
*Relevance:* Theoretical scaffolding for MaayHealth's combined Indic-language UI (surface) + Garbh Sanskar/joint-family-aware logic (deep).

**10. Barrera, M., & Castro, F. G. (2006).** *A heuristic framework for the cultural adaptation of interventions.* **Clinical Psychology: Science and Practice, 13(4), 311–316.**
*Key finding:* Four-stage adaptation methodology (information gathering → preliminary design → test → refinement) with three equivalence criteria.
*Relevance:* Concrete adaptation methodology MaayHealth's iterative testing with mothers, ASHAs and mothers-in-law should follow.

**11. Sambasivan, N., Checkley, G., Batool, A., et al. (2018).** *"Privacy is not for me, it's for those rich women": Performative privacy practices on mobile phones by women in South Asia.* **SOUPS 2018, USENIX, pp. 127–142.**
*Key finding:* 199 South Asian women reported family surveillance of their phones and used five "performative privacy" tactics to manage it.
*Relevance:* Direct foundation for MaayHealth privacy patterns — app locks, ambient-safe voice prompts, and discreet sensitive-topic flows.

**12. Kumar, N., & Anderson, R. J. (2015).** *Mobile phones for maternal health in rural India.* **CHI 2015, pp. 427–436.**
*Key finding:* Rural Indian women's mobile use is gatekept by husbands and in-laws; appropriation happens through community/group viewing.
*Relevance:* MaayHealth must design for community/intermediated use rather than assume a private 1:1 user-app relationship.

**13. Ismail, A., & Kumar, N. (2021).** *AI in global health: the view from the front lines.* **CHI 2021, Article 587.**
*Key finding:* AI in LMIC health suffers a "streetlight effect" — targeting measurable surveillance over relational frontline work; risks displacing CHWs.
*Relevance:* Critical caution that MaayHealth must augment, not replace, the ASHA relationship.

**14. Lunkenheimer, H. G., Burger, O., Akhauri, S., et al. (2021).** *Tradition, taste and taboo: the gastroecology of maternal perinatal diet.* **BMJ Nutrition, Prevention & Health, 4(2).**
*Key finding:* Bihari mothers' food choices fuse Ayurvedic, biomedical, and family-influencer logics; mothers-in-law outweigh ASHAs in influence.
*Relevance:* Empirical map of which traditional Indian beliefs MaayHealth should reinforce vs. carefully reframe.

**15. Longvah, T., Ananthan, R., Bhaskarachary, K., & Venkaiah, K. (2017).** *Indian Food Composition Tables 2017.* **National Institute of Nutrition (ICMR), Hyderabad.**
*Key finding:* Authoritative database of 151 nutrient components for 528 Indian foods analysed by AOAC methods.
*Relevance:* Foundational data layer for MaayHealth's Indian-recipe nutrition reasoning, complemented by Vijayakumar et al. (2024) INDB.

### Theme 3 — Voice-first and low-literacy design

**16. Medhi, I., Patnaik, S., Brunskill, E., Gautama, S. N. N., Thies, W., & Toyama, K. (2011).** *Designing mobile interfaces for novice and low-literacy users.* **ACM TOCHI, 18(1), Article 2.**
*Key finding:* Across 90+ low-literacy users, text-based UIs are unusable; live-operator interfaces are up to 10× more accurate; speech is faster for the speech-comfortable.
*Relevance:* Empirical justification for MaayHealth's voice-first, near-textless interaction model.

**17. Vashistha, A., Cutrell, E., Borriello, G., & Thies, W. (2015).** *Sangeet Swara: a community-moderated voice forum in rural India.* **CHI 2015, pp. 417–426. (Best Paper.)**
*Key finding:* IVR voice forum drew 25,000 calls and 200,000 playbacks from 1,500+ low-income / visually impaired users in 11 weeks.
*Relevance:* Proves voice-only forums can scale meaningfully in rural India — informs MaayHealth's peer-Q&A augmentation of AI.

**18. Mohan, D., Bashingwa, J. J. H., Scott, K., et al. (2022).** *Optimising the reach of mobile health messaging programmes: an analysis of system-generated data for the Kilkari programme across 13 states in India.* **BMJ Global Health, 7(8), e009395.**
*Key finding:* Kilkari reached 21% of target pregnant women across 13 states; only 39% retained — passive one-way voice has scale but engagement decay.
*Relevance:* Defines the retention problem MaayHealth's interactive AI design must beat.

**19. Yadav, D., Malik, P., Dabas, K., & Singh, P. (2019).** *Feedpal: understanding opportunities for chatbots in breastfeeding education of women in India.* **Proc. ACM HCI, 3(CSCW), Article 170.**
*Key finding:* In a Wizard-of-Oz study with 22 Delhi-slum mothers/CHWs, 88% of breastfeeding queries were chatbot-answerable; mothers welcomed private Q&A; mothers-in-law's influence was prominent.
*Relevance:* India-specific peer-reviewed precedent for MaayHealth's exact category — an AI conversational agent for Indian maternal education.

### Theme 4 — AI safety and nutrition

**20. Semigran, H. L., Linder, J. A., Gidengil, C., & Mehrotra, A. (2015).** *Evaluation of symptom checkers for self-diagnosis and triage: audit study.* **BMJ, 351, h3480.** DOI: 10.1136/bmj.h3480.
*Key finding:* Across 23 symptom checkers, the correct diagnosis was first only 34% of the time; appropriate triage 33–78%.
*Relevance:* Establishes the safety baseline for any maternal symptom-checking feature — supports MaayHealth's "AI-assist with mandatory escalation" architecture, particularly for pregnancy red flags. (Reinforced by Wallace et al., 2022, *npj Digital Medicine* 5(1):118; Schmieding et al., 2022, *JMIR* 24(5):e31810.)

## 3. Literature review

### Why digital maternal nutrition matters in India

India's maternal mortality ratio fell to **97 per 100,000 live births** in 2018–20 (SRS, 2022), yet the country still accounts for roughly 8% of global maternal deaths, and inter-state variation is stark — from 19 in Kerala to 284 in Arunachal Pradesh. Nutrition is a central, modifiable driver: anaemia, gestational diabetes, and low gestational weight gain disproportionately affect non-metro Indian mothers. Mobile health (mHealth) is the most plausible channel for closing this gap at scale, given that India's mobile-internet gender gap has narrowed from 40% to 30% (GSMA, 2024), even though only one-third of Indian women have ever used the internet (NFHS-5). The peer-reviewed evidence base, however, is uneven: systematic reviews of pregnancy app interventions report acceptable feasibility but heterogeneous, underpowered effects on outcomes (Daly et al., 2018; Overdijkink et al., 2018), with the strongest effects reported in a meta-analysis by Chan and Chen (2019) for gestational weight management and gestational diabetes control. Two cluster RCTs anchor the antenatal-care (ANC) adherence claim: Lund et al. (2014) reported a 13-percentage-point absolute (≈42% relative) increase in ≥4 ANC visits in Zanzibar, and Bangal et al. (2017) demonstrated significant gains in ANC visits, IFA-tablet consumption, and institutional deliveries in an Indian RCT. Together these studies justify MaayHealth's appointment-reminder, IFA-adherence, and gestational-tracking modules.

### The existing app landscape and its failures

Tripp et al.'s (2014) early audit of >1,500 pregnancy apps already showed an "informational + interactive" model dominating the space, but Hughson et al. (2018) sharpened the critique: pregnancy apps are overwhelmingly designed for English-speaking, high-income, literate users, leaving culturally and linguistically diverse women systematically underserved. The competitive landscape confirms this empirically. Flo Health (380M downloads, 22 languages) and Pregnancy+ (Philips, 14–20 languages) include no Indian languages; Ovia, Glow, and What to Expect are English-only. Even Indian-origin apps such as Healofy and Mylo, while supporting up to nine Indian languages and rich cultural framing (Garbh Sanskar, dadi-ke-nuskhe), are text-first, commerce-led, and lack a structured regional food database. Across all 13 mainstream apps reviewed, none offers voice-first interaction. Tucker et al. (2021) further document the "postpartum cliff": commercial peripartum apps cover a median of only 19.5/30 maternal postpartum topics and are weakly inclusive of women of colour. Engagement evidence from Brusniak et al. (2020) and the BaBBLeS evaluation of Baby Buddy (Deave et al., 2019; Mackintosh et al., 2022) shows that even NHS-backed apps struggle with retention beyond the first trimester, with Baby Buddy retaining only ~38.5% of users at three months. MaayHealth must therefore differentiate not on content alone but on language, voice, cultural adaptation, postpartum continuity, and personalisation.

### Voice-first and low-literacy design as proven necessities

Medhi et al. (2011), in the canonical TOCHI study of low-literacy interfaces, demonstrated that text UIs are essentially unusable for first-time low-literacy users, that live-operator interfaces are up to 10× more accurate, and that speech can be the fastest modality for users comfortable with it. Sherwani et al. (2007) extended this finding to health-information delivery in South Asia, showing that well-designed speech UIs outperform DTMF for both literate and low-literate users on health tasks. At scale, Vashistha et al.'s (2015) Sangeet Swara CHI Best Paper documented 25,000 calls and 200,000 playbacks in 11 weeks from low-income rural Indians, and Patel et al.'s (2010) Avaaj Otalo flagged the practical bottleneck — regional-language ASR was not yet robust. India's two flagship voice maternal programmes provide the strongest indigenous evidence: the mMitra pseudo-RCT in Mumbai (Murthy et al., 2020) reported a **91% satisfaction rate** and significant gains in care-seeking knowledge among low-income mothers, while the Kilkari evaluation (Mohan et al., 2022) — the world's largest such programme, with 16M+ subscribers across 12 Indian languages — confirms that voice messaging can scale, but also that one-way audio retains only 39% of subscribers. MaayHealth's interactive AI voice agent is positioned as the next-generation answer to Kilkari's retention problem, validated by Yadav et al.'s (2019) Indian Wizard-of-Oz study showing that a chatbot could answer 88% of Indian mothers' breastfeeding queries.

### Cultural adaptation, family dynamics, and food

Resnicow et al.'s (1999) two-level framework — surface (language, food, imagery) versus deep (values, history, beliefs) cultural adaptation — and Barrera and Castro's (2006) staged adaptation methodology together provide the theoretical scaffolding for designing health interventions for Indian users. Three Indian-context strands populate the deep-structure layer. First, Lunkenheimer et al.'s (2021) Bihar gastroecology study and Chakrabarti and Chakrabarti's (2019) West Bengal study empirically map which traditional Indian beliefs about pregnancy diet *align* with biomedical advice (and should be reinforced) and which *conflict* (and require non-confrontational reframing). Second, Sambasivan et al.'s (2010, 2018) work on intermediated technology use and "performative privacy" in South Asia — and Kumar and Anderson's (2015) feminist study of rural Indian women's mobile use — demonstrate that pregnant women rarely have private device control; they share phones with husbands and in-laws and engage in elaborate privacy tactics around sensitive content. MaayHealth's interaction patterns must therefore be designed for the joint family, not the individual. Third, the data layer is now solvable: the ICMR-NIN Indian Food Composition Tables (Longvah et al., 2017) and the open-access Indian Nutrient Databank (Vijayakumar et al., 2024) provide nutrient profiles for 528 Indian foods and ~1,000 recipes — enabling accurate nutrition calculations for thalis, dal-chawal, idli, and ragi that Western databases cannot deliver. Ismail and Kumar (2021) supply the necessary caveat: AI in Indian maternal health risks displacing rather than augmenting the trusted ASHA relationship, and must be designed in dialogue with frontline workers.

### AI for symptom checking, food recognition, and personalised nutrition

The AI literature delivers both enthusiasm and warnings. Symptom-checker audits — Semigran et al. (2015), Schmieding et al. (2022), and the npj Digital Medicine systematic review by Wallace et al. (2022) — consistently find first-listed diagnostic accuracy of 19–38% and uneven triage accuracy, with five-year follow-up showing limited improvement. Singhal et al.'s (2023) Med-PaLM exceeded the USMLE passing mark, but its own authors flagged residual safety gaps; Gilbert et al. (2024) outline the regulatory standards LLM health apps must clear. For MaayHealth, this evidence converges on a "guided-information layer with mandatory escalation pathways" architecture, not autonomous diagnosis — particularly for low-literacy users who cannot independently detect hallucinations. On nutrition, Im2Calories (Meyers et al., 2015), NutriNet (Mezgec & Koroušić Seljak, 2017), FoodNet (Pandey et al., 2017), and the YOLOv4 thali-recognition work by Pratapa et al. (2022) show that domain-specific food-image recognition is feasible at ~500-class scale and that the central Indian challenge — multiple co-occurring dishes on a single plate — is actively being solved. The MyFitnessPal validation by Bhatt et al. (2020) cautions that micronutrient accuracy (iron, folate) is precisely where consumer apps fail — the macronutrients matter for weight, but the micronutrients matter for maternal anaemia. Personalised-nutrition foundations from Zeevi et al. (2015) and Berry et al. (2020) justify individualised meal recommendations over one-size-fits-all advice. Finally, GDm-Health (Mackillop et al., 2018) confirms the non-inferiority of mobile-mediated remote monitoring in gestational diabetes, while the BUMP trials (Tucker et al., 2022) deliver a sober reminder that telemonitoring without integrated clinical-decision pathways does not, by itself, improve hypertension detection in pregnancy.

### Synthesis — how the evidence shapes MaayHealth's design

MaayHealth's design choices are not stylistic preferences; each is evidentially anchored. **Voice-first, multilingual interaction** is mandated by Medhi et al. (2011) and Sherwani et al. (2007) at the UX level and by mMitra (Murthy et al., 2020) and Kilkari (Mohan et al., 2022) at the population-health level. **Two-way AI conversation** is the documented antidote to Kilkari's 39% retention ceiling (Mohan et al., 2022) and is feasible in the Indian context (Yadav et al., 2019). **Deep cultural adaptation** — Garbh Sanskar framing, joint-family-aware advice flows, and engagement with traditional food beliefs rather than against them — is grounded in Resnicow et al. (1999), Barrera and Castro (2006), Sambasivan et al. (2018), Kumar and Anderson (2015), and Lunkenheimer et al. (2021). **A structured Indian regional food database** is now a solved data problem (Longvah et al., 2017; Vijayakumar et al., 2024) and is the principal feature gap in every competitor app surveyed. **AI nutrition tracking via thali photography** has technical precedent (Pratapa et al., 2022; Pandey et al., 2017) and a clear validation strategy (Bhatt et al., 2020). **AI safety guardrails with human escalation** are demanded by Semigran et al. (2015), Wallace et al. (2022), Singhal et al. (2023), Gilbert et al. (2024), and Ismail and Kumar (2021), the last of whom further requires that MaayHealth augment — not displace — ASHAs and clinicians. **Postpartum continuity** addresses the cliff documented by Tucker et al. (2021) and Deave et al. (2019). In short, MaayHealth is positioned not as a feature-additive Indian variant of Healofy or Flo, but as the first system to integrate the voice-first scale of Kilkari, the cultural depth of Indian-context HCI4D research, the data accuracy of ICMR's food-composition science, and the personalisation power of contemporary AI — within the safety envelope the digital-health literature now demands.

## Conclusion — what the evidence base makes possible

The reviewed literature collectively shows that the components MaayHealth needs are individually proven but have never been integrated. Voice-first delivery works at population scale (Kilkari, mMitra), conversational AI is acceptable to Indian mothers (Yadav et al., 2019), Indian-cuisine vision is a tractable ML problem (Pratapa et al., 2022), Indian nutritional data is open and authoritative (Vijayakumar et al., 2024), and cultural-adaptation theory is mature (Resnicow et al., 1999; Barrera & Castro, 2006). What no existing app does is connect them. The dominant Indian apps (Healofy, Mylo) chose multilingual text + commerce; the dominant global apps (Flo, Pregnancy+, Ovia) chose Western tracking + scale; the dominant programmes (Kilkari, mMitra) chose voice + reach but stopped at one-way IVR. MaayHealth's contribution — and its UIUDP justification — is to demonstrate that an interactive, voice-first, AI-personalised, culturally-grounded maternal nutrition system for Indian mothers is now both technically and evidentially feasible, and that the engagement and equity gaps documented across the field can plausibly be closed by combining what the literature has, until now, only studied in isolation.
