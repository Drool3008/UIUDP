# **Introduction** {#introduction}

Informal workers in India are individuals engaged in employment that lacks formal contracts, social security benefits, and legal recognition. They make up around **90% of the country's labour force** (as of 2020), and about **80% in urban areas like New Delhi**. This includes a wide range of professions such as **domestic workers, home-based workers, street vendors, waste pickers, and construction labourers**. To make ends meet, many of them work **over 50 hours a week**, often earning only minimum wage. Most fall in the age range of **25 to 53 years**.

**Personal financial planning** involves managing income and expenses to ensure long-term financial well-being. It usually includes areas like **cash flow management, tax planning, investments, risk coverage, estate planning, and retirement planning**. However, for informal workers surviving on daily or weekly wages, **complex financial areas like tax or estate planning are often irrelevant**. Instead, they require **practical, daily financial skills** like budgeting, saving, and **responsible borrowing** making informed loan choices based on repayment ability.

Despite its importance, **India’s financial literacy rate is only 42.8%**, and even lower among the informal sector. This leads to poor financial decisions, **risky borrowing**, and **no long-term financial growth**.

To address this, we propose a solution that uses **gamification** to teach financial literacy in a way that fits into their **busy and demanding lives**. Using **micro-lessons, simple games, and community engagement**, we aim to make learning quick, enjoyable, and actionable.

Our research focuses on **how informal workers earn, spend, save, and borrow money**, the **challenges** they face, and the **apps and games** they already engage with. We also explore the **gamification strategies** that work best for this audience to help them gain financial confidence and make better decisions.

# **Literature Review** {#literature-review}

To build a strong foundation for our research, we began with an extensive literature review. We explored key concepts such as **informal workers**, **financial planning**, **responsible borrowing**, and **gamification frameworks**. We also looked at various **theoretical models** related to **cognition**, **user interaction**, **UX design**, and **usability principles** to understand how they could be applied to our solution.

## **Informal Workers** {#informal-workers}

Informal workers in India make up more than **90% of the workforce**, yet their jobs often lack formal contracts, job security, social benefits, and legal recognition. Their employment is usually based on verbal agreements, leading to **low wages, job instability, and poor working conditions**. These workers include **street vendors, domestic workers, construction laborers, agricultural workers**, and many self-employed people running **unregistered businesses**.

Their economic vulnerability is compounded by limited financial resources and lack of protection under labor laws. Many face occupational hazards, but without safety nets or health coverage.

In **rural areas**, informal work mostly centers around agriculture, with people working seasonally or casually in low-paying jobs. In **urban areas**, informal workers are found in construction, domestic work, street vending, and small-scale industries. Migrant workers often take up these jobs, usually on a **temporary basis**, without any formal benefits or social security.

Although some policies like the **Unorganized Workers’ Social Security Act (2008)** and schemes such as **PM-SYM** (for pensions) and **PMKVY** (for skill development) exist, they **suffer from weak implementation**. The **scale and diversity** of the informal sector make it difficult for these initiatives to reach everyone effectively.

To support financial literacy among informal workers, researchers have suggested **community-based programs**, **mobile learning campaigns**, and **microfinance or self-help groups**. These not only teach financial basics but also encourage savings and responsible credit use. However, with financial literacy among informal workers estimated at just **27%**, much more needs to be done to make these interventions effective.

## **Financial Planning** {#financial-planning}

**Financial planning** is essential for making informed money decisions, but most informal workers lack access to the tools or knowledge to do this effectively. With just **42% of the population** financially literate, many informal workers **struggle to use digital tools** like mobile banking or UPI payments. These tools could support their financial inclusion, but **lack of awareness and training** prevents widespread adoption.

To address this, models like **GETU** (Government, Education, Training at Home, Upskilling at College) have been proposed to improve literacy. These can be adapted into **interactive or gamified learning modules** to help workers learn about budgeting, saving, and borrowing in a simple and fun way.

Poor financial planning often leads to **mental stress, anxiety, and debt**, especially among informal workers who have **irregular incomes** and few savings. Gamified tools can help ease this by turning financial concepts into **interactive games**, making it easier to learn about managing income, planning for emergencies, or setting financial goals.

For example, simulation-based games can mimic real-life situations like dealing with **fluctuating income**, helping users develop practical skills like **saving** and **budgeting**. These tools can also explain complex ideas like **risk diversification** and **investment strategies** in simple ways that informal workers can relate to.

In cities, local governments deal with financial planning through **budgeting and resource allocation**. Informal workers, however, often depend on **informal loans**—with about **42.9%** borrowing outside formal channels. Gamification can teach responsible borrowing through role-play scenarios, allowing workers to compare loan options and **avoid high-interest traps**, boosting their financial resilience.

## **Responsible Borrowing** {#responsible-borrowing}

Being financially literate is linked to **responsible borrowing**. People who understand loan terms are less likely to fall into debt traps and are better at comparing loan products like credit cards or mortgages. In contrast, rural and low-income communities especially those with lower education levels are more vulnerable to **high-cost, unregulated loans**.

In places where there is little financial regulation, literacy acts as a **shield**, helping people avoid **predatory lenders**. When loan terms are clearly disclosed, the need for financial literacy reduces slightly, but it still plays a role in helping people understand the **true cost** of borrowing.

Research also shows that responsible borrowing habits are more common among **higher-income** households and those with a strong sense of **financial capability**. For lower-income groups like informal workers, providing financial education especially through **interactive or game-based learning** can fill this gap and support better decision-making.

## **Gamification** {#gamification}

**Gamification** uses game-like elements—such as rewards, challenges, and progress tracking—in non-game settings to **motivate and engage** users. It’s increasingly being used in fields like education and finance to make learning more enjoyable and effective.

Three major gamification frameworks guide the design of such systems:

* The **6D Framework** outlines a step-by-step method for defining objectives, understanding users, and choosing the right game mechanics.

* **Self-Determination Theory (SDT)** focuses on internal motivation, emphasizing **autonomy, competence, and connection** as key drivers of engagement.

* The **Octalysis Framework** breaks down eight motivational triggers, like achievement and curiosity, to make non-game activities more engaging.

Studies have shown gamification to be effective in **learning environments** and **business strategies**. For example, the **Technology Acceptance Model (TAM)** and **Task-Technology Fit (TTF)** frameworks have been used to understand why users adopt gamified systems. Research by **Huang and Hew** found that factors like **ease of use** and **perceived usefulness** are crucial for continued engagement. Similarly, **Werbach and Hunter** highlighted how well-designed game mechanics can **boost motivation and performance**, lessons that apply directly to financial education tools.

By applying these gamification principles to financial literacy for informal workers, we can **transform dull, complex topics into fun, engaging experiences** that are both practical and empowering.

# **Stakeholders** {#stakeholders}

Our primary focus is the **low income population and informal workforce in India** \-\> individuals who are largely excluded from formal financial systems and seek better financial inclusion.

To understand the broader ecosystem surrounding this issue, we identified and categorized stakeholders based on their level of involvement and influence. These stakeholders were classified into three categories:

* **Primary Stakeholders**: These are the main users of the platform \- informal workers such as daily-wage laborers, street vendors, domestic workers, and others in unorganized sectors. They directly interact with the platform and are the key beneficiaries of financial literacy, planning, and responsible borrowing education.

* **Secondary Stakeholders**: These include family members of informal workers, Banks, local community leaders or employers, Micro Financers and educators who support or influence the financial decisions of the primary stakeholders. They may use or advocate for the platform indirectly, helping spread awareness or facilitating access.

* **Tertiary Stakeholders**: This group includes policymakers, government agencies, financial institutions, NGOs, Media. While they do not directly use the platform, they play a critical role in creating enabling environments through policy, funding, partnerships, or insights that can improve the reach and effectiveness of financial inclusion initiatives.

By mapping stakeholders in this layered manner, we aim to design a solution that not only addresses the immediate needs of informal workers but also considers the ecosystem that supports and shapes their financial behaviors.

# **Research Methodology** {#research-methodology}

To develop a deep understanding of the behaviors, needs, and preferences of informal workers, we adopted a **mixed-methods research approach**. This included both qualitative and quantitative methods to ensure a well-rounded perspective.

We conducted **structured interviews** using a combination of closed and open-ended questions. This helped us gather quantifiable data while also allowing participants to share detailed personal insights. In total, we interviewed **26 individuals**, including a **focused group of 10 participants**, to foster in-depth discussions around shared experiences.

To better understand how context influences user behavior, we carried out **contextual inquiries** by visiting workers directly at their places of work. By observing their real-life environments and engaging them in natural conversations, we were able to gain firsthand insight into their daily challenges, tasks, and financial decision-making in situ.

After collecting the data, we moved into a **systematic analysis and synthesis** phase. We looked for recurring patterns, common behaviors, and themes in the responses. These findings were then distilled into **user insights** that directly shaped our understanding of the target audience.

Finally, we created **user personas** \- representative profiles based on the different types of users we encountered. Each persona highlights key traits, goals, challenges, and motivations, helping us design solutions that are grounded in the real needs of our users.

# **User Insights: Pains and Gains from Interviews** {#user-insights:-pains-and-gains-from-interviews}

We conducted two phases of user interviews to understand the financial behaviors, challenges, and aspirations of informal workers in India. Each phase used a mix of structured and unstructured approaches:

* **Phase 1**: On-campus workers (e.g., construction workers) via group discussions and brief interactions, often mediated by a professor.  
* **Phase 2**: Off-campus workers (e.g., auto drivers, vendors) in natural settings during low-customer hours, allowing deeper engagement.

Though both phases shared consistent research goals, Phase 2 provided richer insights due to a more unstructured and organic approach with a more diverse participant base.

## **Key Pains (Challenges Faced)** {#key-pains-(challenges-faced)}

#### **1\. Unstructured Financial Behavior and Irregular Savings**  Most informal workers lack a habit of planned savings. Their financial behavior tends to be reactive rather than proactive, with expenditures often exceeding earnings by the middle of the month. Without any structured approach to setting aside money, they are especially vulnerable during emergencies, lacking any form of financial cushion.

#### **2\. Unstable Income**  Unstable or fluctuating income is a significant issue, particularly for off-campus workers such as drivers and street vendors. Their earnings vary from day to day, making it difficult to plan for consistent expenses. This unpredictability often results in little to no disposable income, contributing to chronic financial insecurity.

#### **3\. Dependence on Informal Lending**  Many workers rely on informal lending sources, such as local moneylenders, who often charge exorbitant interest rates. This leads to recurring debt cycles and considerable repayment stress. Without access to safe and affordable credit, these workers remain trapped in financially precarious situations.

#### **4\. Distrust in Formal Financial Systems**  A big fear of fraud and perceived complexity discourages workers from engaging with banks, digital wallets, and formal loan systems. Many feel alienated or intimidated by these institutions, leading them to avoid beneficial financial products like savings accounts or secure borrowing options.

#### **5\. Poor Spending and Budgeting Habits**  Most workers do not track their daily or monthly expenses. Without basic budgeting skills, money is often spent impulsively, and funds are depleted prematurely. This lack of financial planning severely limits their ability to meet short- or long-term financial goals.

#### **6\. Low Digital and Financial Literacy**  There is widespread hesitation to use digital financial tools like mobile banking apps or UPI services. This reluctance stems from a lack of digital literacy, fear of scams, and a general lack of confidence. As a result, workers miss out on tools that could streamline their financial activities.

#### **7\. Unproductive Use of Free Time**  A significant amount of free time is spent on mobile games or passive entertainment. While this provides short-term enjoyment, it does not contribute to personal development or financial improvement, resulting in lost opportunities for growth or learning.

#### **8\. Unclear Learning Pathways**  Even when motivated to improve, many workers are unsure about what financial skills to learn or where to begin. This uncertainty hampers their progress, as they lack clear, accessible entry points into financial literacy or self-improvement.

## **Key Gains (Opportunities for Impact)** {#key-gains-(opportunities-for-impact)}

**1\. Strong Motivation to Save and Improve Financial Well-Being**  
 Informal workers are highly motivated to save and improve their financial situation, especially when presented with simple, relevant tools. To tap into this motivation, a **"Savings Buddy"** feature could be introduced—this would act as a visual savings goal tracker with gamified elements and motivational prompts. Such a feature can keep workers engaged and incentivize them to develop better financial habits.

**2\. Willingness to Learn and Grow**  
 Workers demonstrate a strong desire for personal and financial growth, which presents an opportunity to offer educational interventions. Leveraging **social and community learning** through peer-led groups can help workers feel more comfortable and confident in engaging with financial education. The platform could facilitate group discussions, knowledge-sharing, and mutual motivation, ensuring that workers learn in a supportive environment.

**3\. Interest in Gamification and Rewards**  
 Workers are already accustomed to mobile games and enjoy competition. This offers a great opportunity to integrate **gamified experiences**, such as daily financial quizzes, saving streaks, and group challenges. These elements would make financial literacy more fun and engaging while encouraging workers to develop sustained financial habits.

**4\. Trust in Social and Community Learning**  
 Peer-led learning is highly effective among informal workers, who trust advice from familiar faces. Creating a **community-driven platform** where workers can share financial tips, success stories, and offer motivation to each other can facilitate the development of a strong support network. This community aspect can significantly boost trust and engagement with financial tools.

**5\. Openness to Simple Tracking Solutions**  
 Many informal workers are willing to use simple, visual tracking tools in their native language. A tool like the **"Budget Master"** \- a gamified feature that simulates daily or weekly income versus expenses—would be a powerful way to increase workers' budgeting awareness and help them better manage their finances.

**6\. Willingness to Explore Safer Borrowing Options**  
 Informal workers are open to transitioning from informal borrowing methods to safer, formal financial solutions if provided with the right education. An **educational initiative on safer borrowing** options, such as explaining the benefits of formal loans and guiding workers toward safer financial choices, can help improve financial resilience and reduce reliance on high-interest loans from informal lenders.

**7\. Build Financial Trust from the Ground Up Using Known Faces and Community Support**  
 To overcome the distrust many workers have toward formal financial systems, we can focus on **trusted community figures** to promote financial literacy. These figures, who are familiar to workers, can help break down barriers to engagement with formal financial services like banks or digital payment systems, fostering a sense of security and trust.

**8\. Turn Everyday Screen Time into Financial Growth Using Reels and Games in the App**  
 Since informal workers already spend significant time on their phones, this can be leveraged to encourage learning. By **integrating financial lessons** into popular formats like short videos or games, we can transform idle screen time into valuable opportunities for financial education. This could help workers develop financial literacy in a fun, engaging, and non-intimidating way.

**9\. Offer Simple, Visual Tools for Budgeting and Planning**  
 To improve budgeting and financial planning skills, we need to offer **easy-to-understand, visual tools**. These could include budget trackers with clear visual cues, helping workers see where their money is going and adjust their spending habits. A simple, user-friendly interface in their preferred language would make such tools more accessible.

**10\. Scam Simulators to Let Them Prepare**  
 Given the prevalent fear of fraud, workers would benefit from **scam simulators** within the app that allow them to recognize potential financial scams. By offering simulated scenarios where they can practice identifying fraudulent offers, workers can become more adept at safeguarding their finances from scams and predatory lenders.

**11\. Create Offline-First, Low-Bandwidth Learning Options with Their Preferred Languages and Options**  
 Recognizing the challenges workers face with internet connectivity, it is important to **develop offline-first, low-bandwidth learning options** that are tailored to their needs. By providing educational content that can be accessed without a continuous internet connection and in their preferred languages, we can ensure that financial literacy is accessible to workers in rural or under-connected areas.

# **Differences in Approach and Insights** {#differences-in-approach-and-insights}

 In Phase 1, interviews were constrained by targeting on-campus workers with fixed incomes, conducted in rushed settings or group discussions mediated by a professor, which limited response depth. In Phase 2, an unstructured approach in off-campus settings allowed for more genuine, detailed responses from a diverse group, though some reluctance to participate persisted. While insights remained largely consistent due to unchanged research objectives, Phase 2 highlighted new pains, such as unstable income and unproductive use of free time, and reinforced the gain of workers’ willingness to learn and grow. These findings underscore the need for accessible, engaging, and community-driven financial literacy tools tailored to the diverse realities of informal workers.

# **Actionable Feature Recommendations and Insights Report** {#actionable-feature-recommendations-and-insights-report}

This section consolidates actionable feature recommendations and insights derived from user research on informal workers and small business owners in India, focusing on their financial behaviors, challenges, and opportunities for intervention. The recommendations address key pain points such as low income, reliance on high-interest informal loans, and limited financial literacy, while leveraging opportunities like community trust and openness to gamified learning. The insights highlight user needs, behavior patterns, pain points, and potential interventions to foster financial inclusion and stability.

## **Actionable Feature Recommendations:** {#actionable-feature-recommendations:}

### **1\. Low Income**

* **Recommendation**: Implement a gamified short-reel-based learning system where users scroll through engaging financial education content while earning virtual points.  
* **Feature Enhancement**: Add interactive quizzes and mini-challenges after each reel to reinforce learning and reward progress, motivating users to stay engaged and complete more lessons.

### **2\. High-Interest Informal Loans**

* **Recommendation**: Develop a community-driven loan pooling system where users invest small amounts into a shared fund, providing low-interest loans akin to crowd-funded micro-lending.  
* **Feature Enhancement**: Create a transparent dashboard that shows real-time updates on contributions, loan distributions, and repayment progress, fostering trust and accountability within the community.

### **3\. Unexpected Expenses**

* **Recommendation**: Introduce an automated micro-saving feature that sets aside a small percentage of income for emergencies, making it easier for users to prepare for financial surprises.  
* **Feature Enhancement**: Incorporate machine learning (ML) to analyze spending patterns and recommend personalized saving amounts, ensuring that users maintain financial stability without sacrificing their day-to-day needs.

### **4\. Fear of Digital Finance**

* **Recommendation**: Create a fraud detection and awareness system that educates users on common scams and flags suspicious messages from SMS, WhatsApp, and social media.  
* **Feature Enhancement**: Implement real-time alerts to notify users of potential scams and introduce a scam-reporting feature that allows the community to share and report fraud attempts, boosting collective awareness.

### **5\. Limited Financial Knowledge**

* **Recommendation**: Enable progressive learning through widgets and notifications that deliver bite-sized financial tips and guidance, tailored to the user’s learning pace.  
* **Feature Enhancement**: Personalize the financial learning experience by adapting the content to the user’s preferences, making sure they are learning in a way that fits their personal context.

### **6\. Income Instability**

* **Recommendation**: Provide a real-time job listing feature that connects users with micro-gigs and small jobs available during idle hours (e.g., waiting for customers, or tasks they can do while working elsewhere).  
* **Feature Enhancement**: Use AI to recommend jobs based on users' skills, location, and availability, helping users maximize their income opportunities, even if they have limited working hours.

### **7\. Limited Investment Knowledge**

* **Recommendation**: Develop a gamified investment learning system simulating real-world investment scenarios, allowing users to practice without real financial risk.  
* **Feature Enhancement**: Create virtual trading competitions where users can compete in investment challenges, earning rewards and gaining practical experience, keeping them motivated to learn more.

### **8\. Lack of Time to Learn and Grow**

* **Recommendation**: Deliver contextual financial education prompts based on user behavior, detecting times of excessive social media or gaming usage, and suggesting relevant learning content.  
* **Feature Enhancement**: Implement automated reminders that encourage users to engage with educational content at optimal times, seamlessly integrating into their daily routines without feeling overwhelming.

## **User Needs & Behavior Patterns** {#user-needs-&-behavior-patterns}

* **Reliance on Cash-Based Transactions**: Users are comfortable with cash due to familiarity and perceived control. They fear hidden charges and fraud with digital transactions, making them hesitant to transition to digital finance.  
* **Microloan & Credit Accessibility**: High reliance on informal lenders with very high-interest rates. There is also fear of rejection from formal banks due to a lack of documentation or a formal credit history, making small business owners vulnerable to exploitative lending practices.  
* **Limited Financial Literacy & Awareness**: Users often do not understand basic financial products such as savings accounts, loans, or insurance. They perceive formal banking as complicated and time-consuming, avoiding it in favor of simpler, less formal options.  
* **Time Constraints & Business Priorities**: Small business owners are focused on their day-to-day operations, leaving little time for financial planning or learning about digital tools. Financial decisions are often made based on peer recommendations rather than formal education or expert advice.

## **Opportunities for Intervention** {#opportunities-for-intervention}

* Bridging the Digital Finance Trust Gap:  
* Localized financial education programs to build trust in digital payments.  
* Simplified, no-hidden-fee digital transaction options.  
* Community-led adoption models where early adopters support others in onboarding.  
* Innovative Microloan Models  
* Flexible, no-collateral loans with AI-based credit scoring using transaction history.  
* Buy Now, Pay Later (BNPL) solutions tailored for small businesses.  
* Peer-group lending models leveraging social credibility instead of traditional collateral.  
* Simplified Digital Banking & Expense Tracking Tools:  
* Mobile-first expense tracking apps with audio-based or vernacular UI for non-tech-savvy users.  
* WhatsApp-based financial tools for easy loan applications and transaction tracking.  
* AI-driven financial assistants offering tailored savings and credit suggestions.  
* Seasonal Financial Products for Small Business Owners:  
* Short-term savings schemes aligned with seasonal income fluctuations.  
* Emergency credit lines requiring minimal documentation to support financial resilience.

# **Observations**

**Target Audience:** The user research we did consists of 26 people in which we had diverse backgrounds and different types of workers like constructor workers, delivery agents, e street vendors, housekeeping staff, cab drivers and others who all come into the category of informal workers around IndiraNagar and IIIT Hyderabad .

There are a lot of insights and new things we experienced when we were conducting interviews.

**Background:** Most people come from Middle class or lower Middle Class families from various parts of India migrated here for employment and to earn bread for their family. All of them had very little education up to 12th at max due to their family conditions and dependency on work.

**Income:** Most informal workers that we interviewed were middle aged men. They are all working to satisfy their daily needs and support their families. 70% to 100% of the income is being spent on their basic needs to live. Workers in IIITH feel better than outsiders as they have a stable income and are assured that they will receive their payment by the end of month. The workers who are working in freelancing jobs like food delivery, cabs or the people having kiosks are dependent on the customers as it's where their income comes from.

**Savings:** Despite having less income and many responsibilities, they still are trying to save money for emergencies. But many of them failed due to lack of knowledge in how to save and how to manage expenses. They either wanted an opportunity to grow or alternative sources of income. They are highly motivated to learn about how to utilize income optimally but having the constraint of less time. They say they had less time but they also contradict this by saying that they frequently play games. So they are seeing their entertainment as part of the work.

**Borrowing:** In situations of urgency of money or shortage of money at the end of month, they usually borrow money informally from their family contacts back at home or friends or the contractors who they work under. They feel that getting a loan from a bank will not be feasible as they are middle class and have no knowledge about the rules and regulations. They think getting money from known persons is better than others even though the interest rate is a bit higher. And also they will go for the less interest rates in the community itself. They think that it enables them to avoid unnecessary violations of rules, paying extra and gives them a flexibility for repayment.

**Scams:** A couple of people mentioned that they got scammed by callers and messages they received. Some people fear scams when thinking of stock markets or having micro-investments.

 They wanted to have a clear distinction between the legit messages / calls and scam calls. Even credit cards seem like a scam to some of them and they don’t want to use credit cards.

**Investments:** They wanted to learn about the investment options and explore the different types of earning methods. But due to the vast content of the investments and the complexity of the investments, they can’t afford that much time on learning as they are very hard working in their daily work. They also can’t understand where to start as they are not that well versed with internet browsing and not educated. They are willing to learn even though they have limited time, so we have to try to provide them knowledge in bytes which won’t consume their time.

**Phone Usage:** Most of them carry smart phones but some of them still use keypads, we should try to accommodate the learning for them too.

**Internet Availability:** As many people pointed, internet connection is also an issue, they can’t just keep connected 24/7. So they needed offline connectivity and synchronized when they connected to the internet. An offline payment option is required.

**Gaming Behaviour:** Most of the workers and shop owners like playing mobile games and they like watching games like Cricket, Football, Kabaddi. Some of the mobile games they play are PUBG, Ludo KIng, FreeFire, Quiz games. So based on these and their opinions on these games, we came to a conclusion that they like the gaming mechanisms that encourage winning, competition, levels and progression, knowledge etc. So We can use methods like Octalysis or SDT, MDA for a learning environment using gamification.

So, By all the Observations that are made by all the above aspects, we got a broader perspective in their financial knowledge and habits and the gratification or gaming technique that many people inclined towards.

These insights came from the mixed method of interview and contextual inquiry by asking them questions about their earning, spending, borrowing and investment habits. These questions helped us to get the insights about their needs, pains and gains, their journey with finance. So based on the observations, these are the needs:

* Informal workers need a simple, structured way to understand financial concepts such  as savings, borrowing, interest rates, and investments.  
* They have limited time but are willing to learn if the information is provided in small, digestible chunks like reels or shorts (e.g. FinanceWithShravan on Instagram).  
* They prefer borrowing from known contacts and fear bank loans due to perceived complexity and lack of knowledge about regulations.  
* There is a strong need for a method to distinguish legitimate financial services from scams and fraud schemes on texts/calls.  
* Many are interested in investments but feel overwhelmed by the complexity and do not know where to start.  
* Since internet connectivity is inconsistent, learning modules should work offline with periodic synchronization when online.  
* The learning solution must be accessible on both smartphones & basic feature phones.  
* Since many of them enjoy playing mobile games, a gamified approach would be an effective way to keep them engaged and motivated to learn.  
* They need a way to experiment with micro-investments in a risk-free manner.  
* A simulation game that teaches investment principles interactively.  
* They need something to remind them to use their time wisely rather than spend it on social media and games.

So to fulfill these needs, we use our user research to use the mental model of the user to change their habits or behaviours into features to meet the needs or to overcome the features. The solution we think of is based on the MDA framework to gamify the product for better reach. It is convenient as there is no requirement of carrying additional equipment with them and they can use it offline. As almost all of them are working physically hard workers, it is good to have less load on physical strength. So based on all the factors, we decided to go with a mobile based solution which is handy and they do not need to spend any additional hardware as many of them already have smart mobiles and also it’s easy to carry.

# Core Features that can aid the participant:  {#core-features-that-can-aid-the-participant:}

**1\. Gamified Learning Modules (Short & Interactive)**

* **Feature Overview**: Engage users with bite-sized, 1-2 minute interactive lessons that explain complex financial concepts in a simple, easy-to-understand manner.  
* **Implementation Details**:  
  * Use real-life scenarios that users can relate to, such as managing income during seasonal fluctuations or deciding whether to save or spend.  
  * After each lesson, users make financial decisions (e.g., saving vs. spending), receiving feedback to understand the impact of their choices.  
* **Impact**: Users can absorb financial knowledge without feeling overwhelmed, and make informed decisions based on their learning.

#### **2\. Financial Simulation & Investment Practice**

* **Feature Overview**: Allow users to create a virtual budget and simulate how their financial choices (e.g., budgeting, spending, saving) will affect their overall financial health.  
* **Implementation Details**:  
  * Provide a risk-free environment where users can experiment with basic investments, such as stock market simulations or savings accounts with compounding interest.  
  * Include a simulated loan system where users can take out loans for educational purposes, which they pay back using in-game points. This helps teach responsible borrowing and repayment.  
* **Impact**: Users practice financial decision-making without the risk of real-world consequences, building confidence in managing their finances and understanding the impact of loans and investments.

#### **3\. Progression & Rewards System**

* **Feature Overview**: A progression system that motivates users to continue learning and improving their financial knowledge.  
* **Implementation Details**:  
  * Users complete financial learning tasks and progress through various levels.  
  * Daily streaks, set learning goals, and in-game currency can be earned by completing lessons. These rewards can be used within the game for simulations or other educational activities.  
* **Impact**: This gamified approach encourages consistency in learning and provides positive reinforcement through rewards, keeping users engaged and motivated to reach their financial goals.

#### **4\. Scam Awareness & Fraud Prevention**

* **Feature Overview**: Teach users how to spot common financial scams and protect themselves from fraud.  
* **Implementation Details**:  
  * Use interactive quiz-trivia to help users differentiate between legitimate and fraudulent financial offers.  
  * Provide real-life examples of scams through videos and game-based scenarios, showing red flags and offering safe practices.  
* **Impact**: Users become more aware of common scams, learn how to recognize warning signs, and build confidence in making secure financial decisions.

#### **5\. Offline Learning & Syncing**

* **Feature Overview**: Ensure that users can continue learning even when they do not have internet access.  
* **Implementation Details**:  
  * Offer modules that can be accessed offline, with progress automatically synced when an internet connection is available.  
  * For users without smartphones, deliver key financial tips via SMS.  
* **Impact**: Users can learn at their own pace and without the need for continuous internet access, making learning more accessible to a wider audience.

#### **6\. Community & Peer Learning**

* **Feature Overview**: Build a supportive community where users can share experiences and learn from one another.  
* **Implementation Details**:  
  * Create local leaderboards and challenges, allowing users to compete with their peers in a friendly way.  
  * Include options for users to share their success stories and financial improvements with others, fostering a sense of community.  
  * Implement chat features where users can discuss strategies and insights with peers.  
* **Impact**: Encourages social learning, where users learn from the successes of others and feel motivated by the shared sense of achievement.

#### **7\. Time Management & Productivity Nudges**

* **Feature Overview**: Help users manage their time and prioritize learning over other less productive activities.  
* **Implementation Details**:  
  * Use reminders and emojis to nudge users to replace excessive gaming or social media time with productive financial learning.  
  * Offer daily learning goals that push users to consistently engage with financial content.  
* **Impact**: Users are gently reminded to focus on financial learning, leading to better time management and more productive use of their time. This helps users build positive habits in managing both their time and finances.

**Mental Model**

**Users' Existing Mental Model**  
Users in the informal sector have specific expectations and habits that decides their interactions with the digital tools in their daily life:

**Technology Use:** Regularly uses social media (Instagram, WhatsApp, YouTube Shorts) and mobile games (Ludo King, PUBG, Free Fire)

**Financial Transactions:** Comfortable with UPI transactions but lacks deep financial knowledge. Uses digital payments when necessary.

**Income & Savings:** Lives on salary to salary monthly, tries to save but struggles due to expenses. Wanted to save but doesn’t know how.

**Borrowing Habits:** Prefers borrowing from known people over banks due to lack of trust and understanding of loans.

**Investments Perception:** See investment as risky or a scam. They don't know where to start.

**Learning Preferences:** Prefers Short, bite-Sized content over long form content, visual and  
Interactive learning works best.

**Time & Learning:** Claims to have no time but spends time on mobile games and entertainment. Prefers short, engaging content.

**Gaming Behavior:** Enjoys games that involve rewards, competition, and levels (PUBG, Ludo, Free Fire).

**Scam Awareness:** Has been scammed or fears being scammed. Wants guidance on identifying fraud.

**Cognitive Effort:** As most of the people still noobs in technology, accounts and others, needed a basic account management system.

**Bridging the Gap**

**Long, complex financial education is boring:** Swiping-based reels, interactive learning in bite-sized videos, interactive choices.

**No motivation to learn finance:** Gamification elements like badges, leaderboards, and streaks.

**Doesn't know how to save money properly:** Users practice budgeting in a virtual game environment.

**Scared of investing due to lack of knowledge:** Users can experiment with risk-free virtual investments.

**Prefers informal borrowing and avoids banks:** Simulated borrowing scenarios to teach responsible borrowing.

**Prefers entertainment over education:** Learning disguised as a game with levels, leaderboards, and badges.

**Falls victim to scams or fears them:** Users identify scams in interactive quizzes and real-life examples.

**Limited or no internet access:** Content is accessible offline, and key tips sent via SMS.
